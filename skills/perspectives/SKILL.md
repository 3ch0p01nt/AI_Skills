# Perspectives Feedback Skill

Draft high-quality Microsoft Perspectives feedback through guided conversation. Mines available data (emails, Teams, meetings, shared work) to build evidence-based feedback, then produces polished output in the official Perspectives format.

## When to Activate

Activate when the user:
- Wants to write a Perspective for a colleague
- Needs to respond to a Perspectives feedback request
- Asks to draft peer feedback for someone
- Mentions Perspectives, peer feedback, or 360 feedback in a Microsoft context

## The Perspectives Format

Microsoft Perspectives uses this exact structure with these exact section headers:

```
Keep doing ...
Here's something I think you do really well and hope you keep doing:
<Your feedback here — specific, evidence-based narrative>

Here's a suggestion for how you could leverage this strength further:
<Actionable suggestion to amplify the strength>

Re-think ...
Here's something you may want to re-think:
<Constructive observation — reframe as opportunity, not criticism>

Here's an example to consider for doing it another way:
<Concrete alternative approach>

Additional thoughts ...
The thing I most value about working with you is:
<Personal, authentic statement about what makes working with them valuable>

Here are some other thoughts I have that you may want to consider:
<Forward-looking advice, career observations, or encouragement>
```

## Intake Workflow

### Step 1: Identify the Subject

Ask the user:
- Who is the Perspective for? (Name, role, team)
- What is their relationship? (Direct collaborator, cross-team, manager, report)
- What timeframe should the feedback cover? (Default: current fiscal year)

### Step 2: Gather Evidence

**If WorkIQ or Microsoft 365 MCP tools are available**, proactively search for:
- Emails between the user and the subject
- Teams chat history
- Shared meetings and meeting transcripts
- Co-authored documents or shared files
- Group collaborations involving both parties

**If data tools are not available**, ask the user:
- "What projects or initiatives did you work on together?"
- "Can you describe 2-3 specific moments where this person made an impact?"
- "What do they do that makes your work better or easier?"

### Step 3: Ask Clarifying Questions

**Do NOT ask all questions at once.** Ask 2-3 per round, prioritized by impact.

#### Priority 1 — Required (always ask if missing)

1. **Specific Collaboration**: "What's the most impactful thing you worked on together? What did they specifically do?"
2. **Strength**: "What's their standout capability — the thing they do better than most?"
3. **Tone Preference**: "Should this be purely positive, or do you want constructive growth suggestions included?"

#### Priority 2 — High Value (ask if not inferrable)

4. **Cross-Team Impact**: "Did their work impact teams beyond your own? How?"
5. **Enterprise Thinking**: "Did they approach problems with an enterprise/scalable mindset vs. point solutions?"
6. **Growth Observed**: "Have you seen them grow or evolve during this period?"

#### Priority 3 — Nice to Have

7. **Specific Quote or Moment**: "Is there a specific conversation, meeting, or deliverable that stands out?"
8. **What You'd Miss**: "If they left the team tomorrow, what would you miss most?"

#### When to Stop Asking

Stop asking and draft when you have:
- At least 2 specific collaboration examples with concrete details
- A clear picture of their primary strength
- Enough context to write the STAR pattern (Situation → Task → Action → Result)
- Understanding of the user's desired tone (positive-only vs. constructive)

### Step 4: Draft the Perspective

Apply these quality principles when writing:

## Writing Quality Framework

### What Makes Great Perspectives Feedback

Based on analysis of high-quality Perspectives feedback at Microsoft:

#### 1. Specificity Over Generality
- **Bad**: "Jon is a great collaborator."
- **Good**: "When the DoW customer needed to integrate generative AI with Microsoft 365 data across government clouds — with no established technical path and multiple organizations involved — Jon stepped in as the architectural connective tissue."

#### 2. STAR Method (Situation → Task → Action → Result)
Every key point should follow this arc:
- **Situation**: Set the context (customer challenge, org gap, technical problem)
- **Task**: What needed to happen
- **Action**: What the person specifically did (strong verbs: architected, drove, surfaced, orchestrated, translated)
- **Result**: Measurable or observable outcome

#### 3. Impact Framing
- Quantify when possible (endpoints, dollars, percentage improvement, time saved)
- Show both customer impact AND Microsoft impact ("both sides of the coin")
- Connect individual actions to enterprise-level outcomes

#### 4. Strength Amplification in "Keep Doing"
The best "Keep doing" sections:
- Name the superpower explicitly ("enterprise approach to problem-solving", "architectural connective tissue")
- Give 2-3 specific examples that demonstrate the pattern, not just one
- Show how the strength creates value at scale, not just in one instance
- End with a concrete suggestion for leveraging the strength further

#### 5. Constructive "Re-think" (When Used)
The best "Re-think" sections:
- Frame as opportunity, never as criticism
- Acknowledge the positive intent behind the current behavior
- Provide a concrete alternative, not just "do better"
- Keep it brief — one observation, one suggestion

#### 6. Authentic "Additional Thoughts"
The best closing sections:
- Are genuinely personal — what would you actually miss if they left?
- Look forward — career trajectory, growth potential, future impact
- Reinforce the person's unique value proposition to the org

#### 7. Microsoft Culture Alignment
Naturally weave in (don't force) alignment with:
- **Growth Mindset**: Learning, iteration, experimentation, resilience
- **Customer Obsession**: Going beyond the ask, anticipating needs
- **One Microsoft**: Cross-team, cross-org collaboration
- **Diversity & Inclusion**: Creating space for others, mentoring
- **Accountability**: Owning outcomes, following through

### Tone Calibration

#### Default: Positive-Only Mode
The user's default preference is **always positive** Perspectives feedback. When in positive-only mode:
- "Keep doing" section: Rich, detailed, multi-example narrative
- "Re-think" section: Frame as "Here's how you could amplify this strength even further" — NOT a weakness
- "Additional thoughts": Warm, forward-looking, encouraging

#### Constructive Mode (only when explicitly requested)
If the user asks for constructive feedback:
- Keep the ratio 80/20 (positive/growth)
- Frame growth areas as "what would make you even more effective"
- Always pair the observation with a concrete suggestion

### Language Patterns

#### Strong Opening Verbs
Use: excels, drives, accelerates, orchestrates, surfaces, translates, architects, champions, demonstrates, embodies

#### Impact Connectors
Use: "which enabled...", "resulting in...", "directly contributing to...", "preventing...", "accelerating...", "positioning Microsoft to..."

#### Enterprise Framing
Use: "Rather than optimizing for a single...", "consistently framed as a reusable model", "scalable across...", "standards-based approach"

#### Avoid
- Vague praise ("great job", "awesome teammate", "really smart")
- Passive voice ("work was done", "results were achieved")
- Hedging language ("I think maybe", "somewhat", "kind of")
- Repetitive structure (vary sentence openings and lengths)

## Output Format

Present the draft in a clean, copy-paste ready format:

```
Keep doing ...
Here's something I think you do really well and hope you keep doing:
[2-3 paragraphs, narrative style, specific examples with STAR arc]

Here's a suggestion for how you could leverage this strength further:
[1 paragraph, actionable and specific]

Re-think ...
Here's something you may want to re-think:
[1 paragraph — positive reframe or growth opportunity]

Here's an example to consider for doing it another way:
[1 paragraph — concrete alternative]

Additional thoughts ...
The thing I most value about working with you is:
[1-2 paragraphs — personal, authentic]

Here are some other thoughts I have that you may want to consider:
[1 paragraph — forward-looking, encouraging]
```

### Step 5: Review and Iterate

After presenting the draft:
1. Ask if any sections need adjustment
2. Offer to strengthen specific examples with more detail
3. Confirm it's ready to submit

## Length Guidelines

- **Keep doing**: 150-300 words (the meat of the feedback)
- **Leverage suggestion**: 50-100 words
- **Re-think**: 50-100 words
- **Example alternative**: 50-100 words
- **Most value**: 75-150 words
- **Other thoughts**: 50-100 words
- **Total**: 400-800 words

## Examples of Excellence

### Example: Cross-Org Technical Leader
> **Keep doing**: Jon excels at driving cross-team alignment in complex, multi-stakeholder environments. When the DoW customer needed to integrate generative AI with Microsoft 365 data across government clouds — with no established technical path and multiple organizations involved — Jon stepped in as the architectural connective tissue. He rapidly produced architecture options (MCP, Retrieval API, non-Copilot approaches) that gave ISD, CSU, and PG teams a shared frame of reference to align around.
>
> Jon takes an enterprise approach to problem-solving. Rather than optimizing for a single customer or engagement, he consistently framed the GenAI.mil architecture as a standards-based, reusable model that could scale across DoD.

### Example: Technical Talent with Growth Arc
> **Keep doing**: Mike has one of the most impressive professional growth trajectories I've witnessed at Microsoft. He transitioned from a career in the Marine Corps — where technical problem-solving meant something entirely different — into becoming a genuine technical leader and subject matter expert in cybersecurity operations. What makes this remarkable isn't just the distance traveled, but the speed and depth of his growth. He doesn't just learn enough to get by — he masters the material and then teaches it back.
>
> When I provide strategic or technical direction, I've learned to expect something extraordinary: I'll check back in a week and find that Mike hasn't just completed what was asked — he's carried it to the next level, adding depth, documentation, and operational readiness I hadn't anticipated.

### Example: Operational Leader Driving Scale
> **Keep doing**: Brian has taken decisive ownership of IL6 MDE deployment — arguably the most operationally complex security initiative in the Navy's classified environment. What started as a 200-endpoint deployment has scaled to managing 25,000+ endpoints under his technical leadership. He didn't inherit a playbook for this — he wrote it, navigating the intersection of Microsoft product capabilities, Navy operational requirements, and IL6 compliance constraints that few people in the company fully understand.

## Data Sources

When available, leverage these sources for evidence:
- **WorkIQ / Microsoft 365 MCP**: Emails, Teams chats, meetings, files shared between the user and subject
- **Obsidian vault**: Win notes, project articles, people profiles that reference the subject
- **Meeting transcripts**: Specific interactions and contributions
- **User's memory**: Direct observations and experiences the user shares conversationally
